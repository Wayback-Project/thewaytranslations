# Genesis 1 Hebrew Terms — Earth vs Universe vs Waters

Created: 2026-04-08 UTC  
Status: Working lexical brief (source-first)

## Short answer first

- Genesis 1 does **not** use a modern Hebrew word for "universe".
- The key phrase is **"the heavens and the earth"** (השמים ואת הארץ / *ha-shamayim ve-et ha-aretz*), a merism meaning total created order.
- In Genesis 1:2, the text uses **"waters"** (*mayim*), **"deep"** (*tehom*), and an unformed-state phrase (*tohu va-vohu*).

## Key Hebrew terms

1) **הארץ (ha-aretz / eretz)**
- Lexical range: land, ground, earth (context-dependent).
- In many Genesis 1 lines, "land" is often better than "planet Earth" in modern-science terms.

2) **השמים (ha-shamayim / shamayim)**
- Lexical range: heavens, skies.
- Not a technical astrophysics term; usually translated "heavens".

3) **מים (mayim)**
- Basic meaning: waters.
- It is not the ordinary word for abstract "fluid substance"; it's concrete waters.

4) **תהום (tehom)**
- Meaning: deep, abyssal deep.
- Often rendered "the deep"; related to primordial depth imagery.

5) **תוהו ובהו (tohu va-vohu)** in Gen 1:2
- Typical gloss: wild and waste / formless and empty / unformed and unfilled.
- Better understood as unproductive, unordered state language.

6) **רקיע (raqia)** in Gen 1:6-8
- Meaning: expanse/firmament.
- Translation as "heavens" or "expanse" is often preferable to modernized "atmosphere" assumptions.

## Earth vs universe question

- If by "universe" we mean modern cosmology, Genesis 1 is not using that technical frame.
- If by "universe" we mean "all that is made," the Hebrew merism "heavens and earth" can function that way rhetorically.
- So source-close rendering usually keeps **heavens + earth/land** rather than replacing with "universe".

## Waters / sea / ocean / substance question

- Gen 1:2 uses **mayim (waters)** and **tehom (deep)**.
- Gen 1:10 uses "gathered waters" called **seas**.
- The text presents ordered cosmic waters language; it is not giving a modern material-science description.

## Science alignment question

- Friction with modern science often comes from reading Genesis as modern mechanism prose.
- Source-near approach: read as ancient cosmological-liturgical ordering text with theological claims.
- Translation should preserve lexical texture; interpretation layers (scientific/theological) should be separated in notes.

## Practical translation guidance for The Way

1. Keep "heavens" for shamayim in Genesis 1 core lines.
2. Use "land" in selected eretz contexts where planet-language would over-specify.
3. Keep "waters" for mayim and "deep" for tehom.
4. Avoid forcing "universe" unless in commentary, not base translation line.
5. Add notes where readers may confuse ancient cosmology with modern physics claims.
